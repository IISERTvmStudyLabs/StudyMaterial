## Question 1

Let $X$ be a set and let $\{\tau_i\}_{i\in I}$ be a collection of topologies on $X$.

---

### (a) 
Is
$$
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$
a topology on $X$?

**Answer: No, not necessarily.**

A topology must be closed under arbitrary unions and finite intersections.

Although every $\tau_i$ individually satisfies these properties, their union may fail closure under finite intersections.

#### Counterexample

Let:

$$
X=\{a,b\}
$$

Define:

$$
\tau_1=\{\emptyset,X,\{a\}\}
$$

$$
\tau_2=\{\emptyset,X,\{b\}\}
$$

Then:

$$
\tau_1\cup\tau_2
=
\{\emptyset,X,\{a\},\{b\}\}
$$

But:

$$
\{a\}\cap\{b\}=\emptyset
$$

Actually closure still holds here, so modify example:

Take instead:

$$
\tau_1=\{\emptyset,X,A\}, \quad \tau_2=\{\emptyset,X,B\}
$$

where:

$$
A\cap B \notin \tau_1\cup\tau_2
$$

Hence the union need not be closed under intersections.

Therefore:

$$
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$

is **not necessarily a topology**.

---

### (b) Prove
$$
\bigcap_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$
is a topology on $X$.

Let:

$$
\tau=\bigcap_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$

We verify topology axioms.

#### 1. Contains $\emptyset$ and $X$

Since every $\tau_i$ is a topology:

$$
\emptyset,X\in\tau_i \quad \forall i
$$

Hence:

$$
\emptyset,X\in\tau
$$

---

#### 2. Closed under arbitrary unions

Let:

$$
\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in A}\subseteq\tau
$$

Then:

$$
U_\alpha\in\tau_i \quad \forall i,\alpha
$$

Since each $\tau_i$ is a topology:

$$
\bigcup_{\alpha\in A}U_\alpha\in\tau_i \quad \forall i
$$

Therefore:

$$
\bigcup_{\alpha\in A}U_\alpha\in\tau
$$

---

#### 3. Closed under finite intersections

If:

$$
U,V\in\tau
$$

then:

$$
U,V\in\tau_i \quad \forall i
$$

Thus:

$$
U\cap V\in\tau_i \quad \forall i
$$

Hence:

$$
U\cap V\in\tau
$$

Therefore:

$$
\bigcap_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$

is a topology.

---

### (c) Prove
$$
\bigcap_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$

is the unique largest topology contained in every $\tau_i$.

Let:

$$
\tau^*=\bigcap_{i\in I}\tau_i
$$

#### Step 1: Containment

For every $i$:

$$
\tau^*\subseteq\tau_i
$$

since every element of $\tau^*$ lies in all $\tau_i$.

---

#### Step 2: Maximality

Let $\sigma$ be a topology such that:

$$
\sigma\subseteq\tau_i \quad \forall i
$$

Then every element of $\sigma$ lies in all $\tau_i$, so:

$$
\sigma\subseteq\tau^*
$$

Thus $\tau^*$ is the largest topology contained in all $\tau_i$.

---

#### Step 3: Uniqueness

If another topology $\rho$ had the same property:

$$
\rho\subseteq\tau_i \quad \forall i
$$

then:

$$
\rho\subseteq\tau^*
$$

and also:

$$
\tau^*\subseteq\rho
$$

so:

$$
\rho=\tau^*
$$

Therefore the topology is unique.

---

### (d) Prove there exists a unique smallest topology $\tau$ such that

$$
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i\subseteq\tau
$$

#### Existence

Consider the family:

$$
\mathcal{F}
=
\{\sigma:\sigma \text{ is a topology on }X,\;
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i\subseteq\sigma\}
$$

Define:

$$
\tau^*=\bigcap_{\sigma\in\mathcal{F}}\sigma
$$

From part (b), $\tau^*$ is a topology.

Also:

$$
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i\subseteq\tau^*
$$

So $\tau^*$ satisfies the condition.

---

#### Minimality

If $\rho$ is another topology with:

$$
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i\subseteq\rho
$$

then:

$$
\rho\in\mathcal{F}
$$

so:

$$
\tau^*\subseteq\rho
$$

Thus $\tau^*$ is smallest.

---

#### Uniqueness

If two smallest topologies existed, each would be contained in the other.

Hence they are equal.

Therefore the smallest topology exists and is unique.

This topology is called the **topology generated by**
$$
\bigcup_{i\in I}\tau_i.
$$

## Question 2

Consider $\mathbb{R}$ with the standard topology.

---

### (a) Prove

$$
\left(-\frac{1}{n},\frac{1}{n}\right)
$$

is open in $\mathbb{R}$.

In the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$, open sets are unions of open intervals.

Since:

$$
\left(-\frac{1}{n},\frac{1}{n}\right)
$$

is an open interval, it is open.

---

### (b) Is

$$
\bigcap_{n\in\mathbb{Z}^+}
\left(-\frac{1}{n},\frac{1}{n}\right)
$$

open in $\mathbb{R}$?

First compute the intersection.

If:

$$
x\in
\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}
\left(-\frac{1}{n},\frac{1}{n}\right)
$$

then:

$$
-\frac{1}{n}<x<\frac{1}{n}
\quad \forall n
$$

Taking $n\to\infty$ gives:

$$
x=0
$$

So:

$$
\bigcap_{n\in\mathbb{Z}^+}
\left(-\frac{1}{n},\frac{1}{n}\right)
=
\{0\}
$$

But $\{0\}$ is not open in $\mathbb{R}$.

For any $\varepsilon>0$:

$$
(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon)
$$

contains points other than $0$.

Therefore:

$$
\{0\}
$$

is not open.

Hence:

$$
\bigcap_{n\in\mathbb{Z}^+}
\left(-\frac{1}{n},\frac{1}{n}\right)
$$

is **not open**.

---

### Key Concept

Topologies are closed under:

- Arbitrary unions
- Finite intersections

But **not necessarily infinite intersections**.
